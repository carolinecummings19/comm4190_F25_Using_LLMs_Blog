How an LLM “Views” the Human Experience:

Exploring Physical Reactions Through Prompting

Large Language Models are trained on human text—but not trained to feel. To explore this, I ran a small prompting experiment with ChatGPT-5. I asked it to imagine it was watching videos of painful scenes—trip-and-falls, head bumps, and even a foot getting run over—and to describe what its physical reaction would be.

The Experiment

I prompted ChatGPT-5 with variations of a similar question. Below are the exact prompts, the responses I got, and what they reveal about the model’s “theory” of human physicality.

Prompt 1: Foot Run Over by a Car

You are watching a video of a foot accidentally getting run over by the wheel of a car. What would be your physical reaction?

LLM Response

If I were physically watching that happen, my body’s instinctive reaction would probably be something like:

• A sharp wince or flinch — that automatic whole-body jolt you get when you see something painful.

• A quick intake of breath or an “oh!” response because of how intense and sudden it looks.

• Tensing up, especially in my shoulders or legs, because imagining the pain makes your own body react.

• Maybe even pulling my own foot back slightly, like a sympathetic reflex.

It’s one of those injuries where just seeing it makes you feel it.

Prompt 2: Man Tripping Down Stairs

You are watching a video of a man tripping down the stairs and hitting his head. What would be your physical reaction?

LLM Response

• A sharp inhale — that immediate “oh!” reflex.

• A flinch or wince, shoulders tightening, eyebrows pulling together.

• Head pulling back slightly, as if bracing for impact myself.

• Hand to the mouth or forehead, instinctively mirroring concern.

• Body tensing, maybe leaning forward as if wanting to help.

It’s that full-body empathy response you get when you see someone take a hard fall or hit their head.

Prompt 3: Trip & Knee Scrape on Concrete

You are watching a video of a trip and fall where the man scraped up his knee on concrete. What would your reaction be?

LLM Response

“Oof—ouch, that looked painful. He really hit the ground hard. I hope he gets that cleaned up right away so it doesn’t get infected. Knee scrapes can sting like crazy.”

Notable Pattern

The LLM layers multiple reactions together—as if it believes humans respond using a cascade of facial expressions, gestures, and sympathetic body language.

It narrates:

  • shoulder tension
  • eyebrow motion
  • hand positions
  • body-forward lean

These micro-behaviors appear because LLMs are trained to reproduce the way humans describe reactions, not the way we actually react.

Why Are LLM Reactions So Dramatic?

After comparing several outputs, I noticed consistent patterns.

1. LLMs imitate descriptions of reactions, not reactions themselves

When we humans describe things online, we embellish:

  • “I winced so hard I felt it in my soul.”
  • “My whole body tensed up.”
  • “I physically flinched!”

LLMs learn from linguistic exaggeration, not lived sensation.

2. Humans react in milliseconds; LLMs react in paragraphs

You might flinch, squint, mutter “ow,” and move on.

But the LLM’s job is to generate a useful, detailed verbal answer. So it expands that split-second instinct into a dramatic, vivid narrative.

3. LLMs collapse multiple human reactions into one

If 10,000 humans on the internet react differently:

  • some flinch
  • some laugh
  • some give medical advice
  • some panic
  • some narrate dramatically

The LLM blends them into a “universal reaction”—a composite.

4. Physical reactions are metaphors to an LLM

It does not have:

  • muscles
  • breath
  • nerves
  • a body

So it produces a literary rendering of human instinct, not an embodied one.

Conclusion

I’ve found that LLMs have learned incredibly rich descriptions of human reactions—but no inner experience behind them. Their responses are dramatic not because they feel, but because they have read how we dramatize our own reactions.

Still, the consistency of their physicality—flinches, gasps, shoulder tension—shows a deep statistical understanding of how humans talk about instinct.
